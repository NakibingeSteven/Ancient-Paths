Antichrists not Antichrist

The word antichrist or its plural antichrists appears only five times in Scripture in two small books before the Book of Revelation. The two little books are 1st and 2nd John. The exact verses are 1 John 2:18, 22; 4:3; and 2 John 7.

The scriptures portray not only a singular antichrist, but multiple antichrists. As stated in 1 John 2:19, these numerous antichrists “went out from us,” signifying their origin from within the Christian community!

This encompasses all that God's Word explicitly communicates about "antichrist," "antichrists," and "the spirit of antichrist." It's crucial to grasp that none of these insightful passages direct us towards a single future malevolent leader.

Confusions from other descriptions

The Holy Scriptures unveil other prophecies concerning the rise of an evil "little horn" (Daniel 7:8), a fearsome "beast" (Revelation 13:1), and "the man of sin" (2 Thessalonians 2:3). It is predominantly these prophecies that are currently being interpreted to endorse the concept of a future malevolent figure.

The clear trajectory of the Bible's literal "antichrist" passages unequivocally points to present realities that true Christians must confront and overcome. So, why would prophecies about the "little horn," "the man of sin," and "the beast" suddenly deviate from this trend and pertain to circumstances Christians will not encounter as they will first be taken away in the rapture?

Denying the nature of Christ

The Bible explicitly mentions on two occasions that rejecting the idea of “Jesus Christ coming in the flesh” is a clear indication of antichrist.

Any spirit acknowledging that Jesus Christ has taken human form is aligned with God, while a spirit that denies this truth is not of God. This denial represents the spirit of the Antichrist, a phenomenon already present in the world (1 John 4:2-3).

Numerous deceivers have emerged globally, refusing to acknowledge Jesus Christ's manifestation in human form. Such individuals are both deceivers and antichrists (2 John 7).

The Human Nature of Christ

When John mentions “Jesus Christ coming in the flesh,” he refers to the concept of “The Word becoming flesh and dwelling among us” (John 1:14). Here, “The Word” denotes Jesus Christ before His birth in Bethlehem, signifying the infinite Son of God taking on human form to live among humanity.

In what manner did Jesus assume human form when He united with humanity?

Paul answered with the utmost clarity: “Forasmuch then as the children are partakers of flesh and blood, He also Himself likewise took part of the same” (Hebrews 2:14 KJV, emphasis added).

The term "children" doesn't pertain to Adam and Eve, as they were not infants but were directly created by God in the Garden of Eden. Instead, it refers to their descendants after sin entered the world, representing fallen humanity.

How is the earthly nature of Jesus related to the concept of antichrist?

The term "the flesh" is a biblical phrase referring to our fundamental human nature influenced by sin. Jesus Christ voluntarily adopted this identical human nature to connect with us, comprehend our challenges, undergo our temptations, and meet us where we stand.

Despite Jesus embracing our flawed human nature and experiencing our humanity, He never engaged in wrongdoing. He faced temptations in every aspect as we do, yet He remained without sin (Hebrews 4:15). Despite His genuine association with human flesh, Jesus, in His thoughts, emotions, and character, maintained a state of holiness, harmlessness, purity, and separation from sinners (Hebrews 7:26).

By assuming our human nature without engaging in its sinful aspects, Jesus effectively denounced sin within human existence (Romans 8:3). Furthermore, He sacrificed Himself for all our sins, those instances where we succumbed to temptation (refer to 1 Corinthians 15:3).

Therefore, in all things, He had to be made like His brethren, that He might be a merciful and faithful High Priest in things pertaining to God…For in that He Himself has suffered, being tempted, He is able to aid those who are tempted (Hebrews 2:17-18)

Subtle Denial of the Nature of Christ

Does the Roman Church genuinely confess “Jesus Christ has come in the flesh”? On the surface, it claims to do so, but let’s take a closer look. One of the official doctrines, or “confessions,” of the Church of Rome is a dogma called The Immaculate Conception.

The Immaculate Conception

Contrary to what many think, this does not refer to the birth of Jesus as a sinless baby, but rather to the conception of Mary herself inside her own mother’s womb. The dogma teaches that when Mary was conceived, she was miraculously preserved from all original sin.

Thus, her nature was “immaculate,” or sinless, and therefore fundamentally different from ours. In other words, according to papal teaching, Jesus’ mother did not have exactly the same flesh and nature that we have.

Implications of the Immaculate Conception

According to papal Rome’s official statements, when Jesus Christ was born, He took Mary’s supposedly sinless nature, not ours. If you think about it, this is really a denial that “Jesus Christ has come in the flesh” (1 John 4:3)!

According to the Roman Church, when Jesus Christ was born, He took Mary’s perfect nature, not our fallen one.

Denying Jesus Came in the Flesh

This doctrine of The Immaculate Conception with its idea of the entrance of Jesus into Mary’s sinless nature, not ours, really removes Christ “one gigantic step away from mankind.

Consequences of the Immaculate Conception

As a result of this dogma and others, Rome doesn’t teach sinners to “be direct” by going straight to our heavenly Father through His Son Jesus Christ for free forgiveness.

Rome declares that by virtue of Mary’s Immaculate Conception and supposedly sinless life, she now has a “saving office” in addition to her Son. Through Mary’s “manifold intercession,” she is now our

“Advocate, Helper, Benefactress, and Mediatrix” who can “bring us the gifts of eternal salvation” and “deliver our souls from death Catechism of the Catholic Church, 252.

Thus, in her official teachings, in spite of appearances, the Roman Church really denies “Jesus Christ has come in the flesh” (1 John 4:3, emphasis added), that is, in “the same” (Hebrews 2:14, emphasis added) fallen flesh the rest of us have.

Denying Jesus' Nature in Paul’s Time

According to the Bible, “the spirit of the Antichrist” was “already” working in John’s own day (church history records a group called Gnostics who denied Jesus Christ’s coming in the flesh in the first century). As history progressed, the same spirit later reared its head through the Immaculate Conception idea.

System not man described by John

Therefore, the idea of only one person as the Antichrist fails the biblical test.

Paul’s Singular Descriptions and their pluralistic interepretation

Paul used various phrases in 2 Thessalonians chapter 2 to describe the Antichrist, such as “the son of perdition” (vs. 3), “the mystery of lawlessness” (vs. 7), and “that Wicked” (vs. 8 KJV) but a careful study of 2 Thessalonians 2 reveals the impossibility of “the man of sin” applying to only one person. Paul stated that in his own day, this “mystery of lawlessness [was] already at work” (vs. 7).

  1. Time of action: This predicted Antichrist was already active in the first century, and Paul emphasized that this “mystery” would continue until the second coming of Jesus Christ (vs. 8). How could this refer to only one human being? He would have to be 2,000 years old.
  2. Corporate use of man: Paul used the expression “the man” in 2 Timothy 3:16-17 in a way that does not refer to one individual but to a succession of godly men throughout history who become “complete” through obeying the Word of God.
  3. In Romans 13:4 (KJV), Paul also used the phrase “the minister of God” to refer to all civil officers throughout history whom God uses to restrain evil.
  4. According to Paul’s own writings, “the man of sin” (2 Thessalonians 2:3) may properly apply to a historical succession of individuals who follow tradition above the Word of Truth.

Daniel and the kingdom/King interpreations of horns and beasts

In Daniel’s parallel prophecy, this same abominable horror is called a little “horn” (Daniel 7:8), and in the Book of Revelation, it is labeled “the beast” (Revelation 13:2). The question arises: Do these apply to only one “Evil Dude” or do they point to something wider and deeper?

  1. A Beast: An angelic interpreter explained to Daniel, “…the fourth beast shall be a fourth kingdom on earth” (vs. 23). The beast represents a kingdom; hence, the Antichrist, who is a beast, must be a collection of people, not an individual.
  2. A Horn: The ten horns are ten kings who will rule that empire. Then another king will appear, overthrowing three kings (Dan 7:24 GNB). The horns represent ten kings connected with their kingdoms, pointing to a list of kings since men rarely outlive their kingdoms.

So the little horn represents groups of men, antichrists throughout history who oppose God.

In summary, the Antichrist is a system, not a man because:

  • Conservative Bible scholars agree that the man of sin (2 Thessalonians 2), the little horn (Daniel 7), and the beast (Revelation 13:1-10) all represent the same power. In prophecy, a beast always represents a kingdom, not an individual person.
  • The little horn would rule for a period of 1260 years; no individual has lived that long!
  • The man of sin was underground in the days of the apostle Paul and was being restrained. According to 2 Thessalonians 2:8-9, the same man of sin will be destroyed by the brightness of Christ’s coming. There is not a person on planet earth who was alive in the days of Paul and will still be alive when Jesus comes.
  • The expression ‘the man of sin’ would seem to refer to an individual. However, the use of a singular word in plurality appears in other contexts for a succession of persons and not for lone individuals. In 1 Samuel 8:11, the expression ‘the king’ is used of a succession of kings. Finally, 2 Timothy 3:17 uses the expression ‘the man of God’ to describe all Christians of all times.
